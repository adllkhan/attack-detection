\subsection{Logistic Regression}

Logistic Regression achieved accuracy of 0.87 and F1-score of 0.83, establishing the performance floor. Its linear decision boundary proved insufficient to capture complex nonlinear feature interactions. Particularly poor recall was observed for Web Attack and Botnet categories (Recall~$<$~0.75), where attack signatures manifest in subtle higher-order feature combinations. The model's primary value lies in its complete interpretability: the learned weight vectors directly indicate each feature's directional contribution to each class prediction.

\subsection{K-Nearest Neighbours}

KNN achieved moderate performance ($F_1 = 0.88$) but at an inference cost prohibitive for real-time detection. With nearly 700{,}000 training points and 74 features, exhaustive nearest-neighbour search requires $\mathcal{O}(nd)$ distance computations per query. Additionally, KNN's performance degrades in high-dimensional spaces due to the curse of dimensionality, which reduces the discriminative power of Euclidean distance as $d$ increases.

\subsection{Random Forest}

Random Forest achieved $F_1 = 0.94$ and $\mathrm{AUC} = 0.97$, demonstrating the substantial benefit of ensemble methods over single-model baselines. The bootstrap aggregation mechanism effectively reduced variance, and the feature importance rankings aligned closely with domain knowledge. However, storing 200 trees in memory presents scalability challenges for very large training sets.

\subsection{XGBoost}

XGBoost achieved $F_1 = 0.96$ and $\mathrm{AUC} = 0.98$, improving on Random Forest across all metrics. Its regularisation parameters ($\gamma, \lambda$) effectively controlled overfitting. However, training time of 317 seconds is $3.6\times$ longer than LightGBM, limiting suitability for environments requiring frequent model retraining.

\subsection{MLP Classifier}

The MLP achieved accuracy~=~0.96 and $F_1 = 0.95$, with competitive performance but greater training instability than gradient-boosting models. Training curves showed occasional loss spikes attributable to heterogeneous feature scales and mini-batch class imbalance. The MLP's black-box nature and greater hyperparameter sensitivity make it less attractive than LightGBM for production deployment.

\subsection{LightGBM --- Best Performing Model}

LightGBM achieved the best performance across all five metrics: Accuracy~=~0.98, $F_1 = 0.97$, $\mathrm{AUC} = 0.99$, training in 89 seconds. This superiority stems from three algorithmic advantages: (1)~leaf-wise tree growth maximises per-split gain; (2)~GOSS sampling reduces training data while preserving informative gradient signals; (3)~EFB bundles sparse features to accelerate histogram computation. The result is a model that is faster, more accurate, and equally well-regularised compared to XGBoost.

LightGBM achieves AUC~=~0.99, maintaining a True Positive Rate above 0.95 at a False Positive Rate below 0.01---a critical property for operational IDS deployment where small FPR values still translate to thousands of daily false alerts on high-throughput networks. Even a FPR of 0.1\% on a network processing 10~million flows per day generates 10{,}000 spurious alerts, overwhelming analyst capacity. The ability of LightGBM to achieve TPR~=~0.98 at FPR~=~0.005 (0.5\%) represents a viable operational point.

\section{Model Interpretability Results}

\subsection{SHAP Global Feature Importance}

Figure~\ref{fig:shap} presents the mean absolute SHAP values for the top 10 features, computed on a stratified subsample of 10{,}000 test instances using TreeExplainer.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/fig03_shap_importance.png}
\caption{Mean absolute SHAP feature importance for LightGBM. Flow Duration, InPktRate, and OutByteRate are the three most discriminative features for attack classification.}
\label{fig:shap}
\end{figure}

The cybersecurity interpretation of the top features:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flow Duration} (mean $|\phi| = 0.503$): Very short durations ($<$10~ms) correspond to port scan probes; very long durations ($>$300~s) are associated with Slowloris and SlowHTTPTest attacks.
    \item \textbf{InPktRate} (0.441): Values exceeding 5{,}000~pkts/s are strongly diagnostic of volumetric DDoS and DoS~Hulk attacks. Legitimate high-rate transfers are distinguished by their consistent byte sizes.
    \item \textbf{OutByteRate} (0.398): Asymmetrically low values relative to InPktRate signal one-directional attacks; very high values may indicate data exfiltration.
    \item \textbf{Bwd Packet Length Max} (0.312): Unusually large values may indicate exploit payloads; very small or zero values suggest SYN-flood or connection exhaustion attacks.
    \item \textbf{Source Port} (0.285): Certain attack tools use characteristic source port ranges or random ephemeral ports, providing statistical signal at the feature level.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SHAP Dependence Analysis}

SHAP dependence plots revealed several important nonlinear relationships:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Flow Duration} exhibits a U-shaped relationship with attack probability: both very short ($<$50~ms) and very long ($>$200~s) flows carry elevated attack probability, while intermediate values typical of legitimate web sessions show low attack probability.
    \item \emph{InPktRate} increases attack probability monotonically above a threshold of approximately 3{,}000~pkts/s, consistent with volumetric attack injection rates.
    \item \emph{Interaction effects:} The combination of short Flow Duration and high InPktRate is the most strongly diagnostic signature for DoS and DDoS attacks, with interaction SHAP values significantly exceeding the sum of individual contributions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{LIME Instance-Level Explanations}

LIME was applied to 200 randomly sampled test instances. For correctly classified DDoS instances, LIME consistently identified high InPktRate and short Flow Duration as dominant positive evidence, with Bwd Packet Length Max providing secondary corroboration.

Analysis of false-positive cases revealed a systematic pattern: legitimate high-volume file transfers exhibited InPktRate and OutByteRate values within the lower range of DoS attack distributions. This motivates the addition of application-layer protocol indicators and rolling traffic variance features in future model iterations.

Analysis of false-negative cases confirmed that Infiltration and Web attack instances with near-benign flow statistics are the primary source of misclassifications. LIME explanations show no single feature provides strong discriminative signal---the attack signature is distributed across multiple weakly informative features in a way the current feature set does not effectively aggregate.

\section{Unsupervised and Temporal Model Results}

\subsection{Isolation Forest}

The Isolation Forest model, trained in unsupervised mode on benign training flows only, achieved Recall~=~0.73 for attack detection at FPR~=~8.4\% with contamination parameter~=~0.05. While substantially lower recall than the supervised LightGBM model, the Isolation Forest successfully flagged 73\% of attack instances without requiring any labelled training data, demonstrating its value as a complementary component for zero-day threat detection.

\subsection{LSTM Temporal Model}

The LSTM model was applied to sequences of 10 consecutive flows grouped by source--destination IP pair. It achieved $F_1 = 0.94$ for combined DDoS/DoS categories and $F_1 = 0.91$ for PortScan---approximately 2 percentage points above the per-flow LightGBM model for these sequential attack types. However, for application-layer attacks (Web, Botnet, Infiltration), no improvement over per-flow classification was observed, as these attacks do not produce distinctive temporal patterns at the flow sequence level within the 10-flow window.

\section{Proposed System Architecture}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} presents the proposed deployment architecture integrating the LightGBM classifier with enterprise SIEM platforms.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/fig10_cross_validation.png}
\caption{Proposed SIEM-integrated system architecture for real-time network attack detection. The dashed feedback line represents analyst-driven threshold calibration and model retraining triggering.}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

The four layers operate as follows:

\textbf{Layer 1 --- Data Ingestion.} Raw packets are captured from SPAN ports or network taps. CICFlowMeter aggregates individual packets into bidirectional flows and computes the 74 selected features per flow, producing a stream of feature vectors ready for classification.

\textbf{Layer 2 --- Classification Engine.} The trained LightGBM model processes incoming feature vectors in near-real time, producing a class probability distribution and predicted label for each flow. A configurable per-class alert threshold allows security operators to tune the precision--recall trade-off according to organisational risk tolerance.

\textbf{Layer 3 --- Interpretability Module.} For each positive detection, a SHAP explanation is computed via the pre-loaded TreeExplainer, providing a ranked list of features most responsible for the classification decision. This human-readable report accompanies each alert, enabling analysts to rapidly assess plausibility and prioritise investigation.

\textbf{Layer 4 --- SIEM Integration.} Detection events and SHAP explanations are formatted as CEF/LEEF events and transmitted to the SIEM platform via RESTful API. Within the SIEM, alerts participate in correlation rules, automated playbook triggers, threat intelligence enrichment, and incident ticket generation.

\section{Discussion of Results}

\subsection{Validation of Research Hypothesis}

The experimental results provide strong evidence supporting the central research hypothesis. LightGBM achieved $F_1 = 0.97$ and $\mathrm{AUC} = 0.99$, exceeding the predicted thresholds ($F_1 > 0.95$, $\mathrm{AUC} > 0.98$). The SHAP analysis confirmed that the model's decision process is grounded in domain-meaningful network flow attributes, substantially reducing the semantic gap between model outputs and analyst understanding identified by Sommer and Paxson~\cite{sommer2010outside}.

\subsection{Practical Significance}

The combination of high detection accuracy, fast inference, and explainable outputs makes the proposed system directly applicable to real-world network security operations. The SIEM integration architecture provides a concrete blueprint for operationalisation, and the interpretability outputs---SHAP summaries, LIME explanations, feature importance rankings---are directly usable as analyst decision-support tools during alert triage and incident investigation.

\subsection{Limitations}

The primary limitation is the use of a single benchmark dataset generated in 2017 under controlled laboratory conditions. Real-world network traffic exhibits substantially greater diversity and temporal non-stationarity. The model's performance on post-2017 attack techniques---AI-generated traffic, supply-chain compromise, zero-trust evasion---remains to be evaluated. Furthermore, Infiltration detection ($F_1 = 0.885$) highlights the inherent difficulty of identifying attacks that are indistinguishable from benign traffic at the flow-feature level without additional contextual information.

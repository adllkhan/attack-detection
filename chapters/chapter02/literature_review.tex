\section{Overview and Scope}

The field of network intrusion detection has generated an extensive body of research over more than three decades, progressing from early statistical anomaly detectors and expert systems, through the supervised machine learning era of the 2000s and 2010s, to the current frontier of deep learning, ensemble methods, and hybrid architectures. This chapter provides a structured review of the literature most directly relevant to the present research, organised around five thematic areas: benchmark datasets, traditional machine learning approaches, unsupervised and anomaly detection methods, deep learning applications, and hybrid ensemble systems. The review concludes with a synthesis identifying the research gaps that motivate this thesis.

The literature was selected through a systematic search of IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, arXiv, and Google Scholar using the keywords ``network intrusion detection,'' ``machine learning cybersecurity,'' ``anomaly detection network traffic,'' ``deep learning IDS,'' and ``CICIDS dataset.'' Papers published between 2009 and 2025 were considered, with an emphasis on works that provide quantitative performance evaluation on standard benchmark datasets.

\section{Network Intrusion Detection Datasets}

\subsection{Historical Development}

The quality and representativeness of training data is arguably the most critical determinant of intrusion detection system performance. The field has relied on a succession of benchmark datasets, each introduced to address the limitations of its predecessors but each carrying its own set of constraints.

\subsubsection{KDD Cup 99 and Its Limitations}

The KDD Cup 99 dataset, derived from the 1998 DARPA network traffic simulation, was for over a decade the dominant benchmark for IDS research. Generated by simulating nine weeks of network traffic in a controlled US Air Force local area network, it contains approximately 4.9 million connection records labelled as normal or belonging to one of four attack categories: Denial of Service (DoS), Probe, Remote-to-Local (R2L), and User-to-Root (U2R)~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd}. However, it was subsequently demonstrated that approximately 78\% of KDD Cup 99 records are duplicates, and that the attack simulation methodology did not reflect realistic traffic generation patterns. Models trained and evaluated on KDD Cup 99 achieve artificially inflated performance metrics that do not generalise to real environments~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd}.

\subsubsection{NSL-KDD}

Tavallaee et al.~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd} proposed NSL-KDD as a refined alternative, removing duplicate records and rebalancing the dataset to include only non-redundant instances. NSL-KDD contains approximately 148{,}517 training records and 22{,}544 test records, making it more tractable for experimental evaluation. While it has been widely adopted, NSL-KDD still reflects 1998-era attack techniques and network protocols, making it increasingly anachronistic for evaluating defences against modern threats.

\subsubsection{CICIDS2017}

The CICIDS2017 dataset, introduced by Sharafaldin, Lashkari, and Ghorbani~\cite{sharafaldin2018cicids}, represents a major advance in dataset quality and realism. It was generated at the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity using a purpose-built traffic generation framework that simulates authentic user behaviour profiles (web browsing, email, file transfer, streaming, VoIP) combined with real attack tools executed by security professionals. Traffic was captured over five days in July 2017 using CICFlowMeter, a flow-based network monitoring tool that extracts over 80 statistical features per flow. The dataset encompasses 14 distinct traffic classes including seven attack categories, making it the most widely used dataset in contemporary IDS research.

\subsubsection{UNSW-NB15}

The UNSW-NB15 dataset was created at the Australian Centre for Cyber Security using the IXIA PerfectStorm tool to generate synthetic network traffic~\cite{moustafa2015unswnb15}. It contains approximately 2.5~million records with 49 features spanning nine attack categories: Fuzzers, Analysis, Backdoor, DoS, Exploits, Generic, Reconnaissance, Shellcode, and Worms. Its network generation methodology is more sophisticated than KDD-era datasets and includes a broader range of modern attack types.

\subsubsection{CIDDS-001 and Bot-IoT}

Ring et al.~\cite{ring2019survey} introduced CIDDS-001 (Coburg Intrusion Detection Data Set), specifically designed to provide realistic background traffic in a corporate network simulation. Unlike most IDS datasets, CIDDS-001 includes detailed contextual metadata about users, devices, and network services, enabling context-aware detection approaches. The Bot-IoT dataset, developed to address the emerging threat of IoT-targeted attacks, models Botnet, DDoS, DoS, and data theft attacks executed through compromised IoT devices, addressing the particular computational constraints and behavioural characteristics of resource-limited connected devices.

\section{Traditional Machine Learning Methods in Intrusion Detection}

\subsection{Survey of Classical Algorithms}

The comprehensive survey by Buczak and Guven~\cite{buczak2016survey} remains one of the most complete references for traditional ML approaches to cybersecurity. Covering decision trees, Random Forests, Naive Bayes, k-NN, SVMs, and their variants, the survey demonstrates that no single algorithm dominates across all datasets and evaluation metrics. Decision trees offer maximum interpretability but are prone to overfitting on complex, high-dimensional feature spaces. Naive Bayes classifiers are computationally efficient and effective when feature independence holds approximately, but tend to underperform when features exhibit strong correlations---as is common in network traffic data. SVMs achieve strong performance in high-dimensional feature spaces and are theoretically well-grounded, but their quadratic training complexity makes them impractical for large-scale real-time detection.

\subsection{Random Forest as the Baseline Ensemble Method}

Random Forest~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd} has emerged as the gold-standard baseline ensemble method for tabular data classification tasks, including network intrusion detection. By constructing an ensemble of decision trees on bootstrap-sampled data subsets with random feature subsets at each split, Random Forest achieves strong variance reduction without compromising bias. Its built-in feature importance mechanism---based on mean decrease in Gini impurity---provides a direct and computationally inexpensive means of identifying discriminative network flow attributes. In the NSL-KDD evaluation by Tavallaee et al.~\cite{tavallaee2009nslkdd}, Random Forest achieved detection rates exceeding 98\% with false-positive rates below 2\% for the majority of attack categories, establishing it as the primary baseline against which subsequent work is compared.

\subsection{Critical Assessment of ML in Operational IDS}

Sommer and Paxson~\cite{sommer2010outside} provided a foundational and widely-cited critical assessment of the application of machine learning to network intrusion detection in operational environments. Their analysis identified several structural challenges that laboratory evaluations on benchmark datasets fail to capture:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Semantic gap:} ML models learn statistical correlations that may not map onto semantically meaningful security concepts, producing alerts that analysts cannot interpret or verify.
    \item \textbf{High base-rate problem:} Even a false-positive rate of 0.1\% on a network processing millions of flows per day generates thousands of spurious alerts, overwhelming analyst capacity.
    \item \textbf{Dataset representativeness:} Benchmark datasets capture specific environments at specific points in time; their traffic distributions may differ substantially from the target deployment environment.
    \item \textbf{Adversarial manipulation:} Unlike intrusion detection in other domains, network IDS faces a deliberate adversary who can observe the system's responses and adapt their attack strategy to evade detection.
\end{itemize}

These concerns directly motivate the interpretability component of the present research, which aims to close the semantic gap between model predictions and analyst understanding through SHAP and LIME analyses.

\subsection{Recent Advances: Gradient Boosting}

The introduction of XGBoost by Chen and Guestrin and subsequently LightGBM by Ke et al.\ marked a significant advance in the state of the art for tabular data classification. Al~Lail et al.~\cite{allail2023ml} conducted a comparative study of multiple ML algorithms for network intrusion detection, demonstrating that gradient-boosting methods consistently outperform Random Forest, SVM, and neural networks on standard IDS benchmarks when evaluated on realistic, imbalanced datasets. Nordin et al.~\cite{nordin2024aireview} provided a recent comprehensive review of AI-driven detection techniques, documenting the trajectory from statistical anomaly detectors to adaptive ML models and highlighting the growing regulatory and operational importance of model explainability.

\section{Anomaly Detection and Unsupervised Methods}

\subsection{Rationale and Principles}

The fundamental limitation of supervised classification in IDS is its inability to detect attack types not represented in the training data---so-called zero-day attacks. An adversary who develops a novel attack technique not present in any existing dataset will evade any purely supervised classifier, regardless of its accuracy on known threats. This limitation motivates the development of anomaly-based detection approaches that identify deviations from an established model of normal behaviour without requiring labelled attack examples.

Anomaly-based IDS can be categorised into three paradigms: \emph{statistical anomaly detection}, which models normal behaviour using parametric statistical distributions and flags observations that are improbably unlikely under the model; \emph{knowledge-based anomaly detection}, which encodes normal behaviour as explicit rules or specifications derived from domain knowledge; and \emph{machine learning-based anomaly detection}, which learns a model of normal behaviour from data without explicit rule specification~\cite{khraisat2019survey}.

\subsection{Clustering-Based Anomaly Detection}

Kim, Lee, and Kim~\cite{kim2014hybrid} applied k-means clustering to network traffic anomaly detection, demonstrating that unsupervised cluster assignment can separate normal and anomalous flows when combined with appropriate feature selection. The k-means algorithm partitions the feature space into k clusters by iteratively minimising the within-cluster sum of squared distances. Observations assigned to small clusters or located far from their cluster centroids are flagged as potential anomalies. While computationally efficient, k-means clustering is sensitive to the choice of k, the initialisation of centroids, and the scale of features---all of which require careful tuning in the IDS context.

\subsection{Density-Based and Statistical Methods}

Zhang and Zulkernine~\cite{zhang2006anomaly} explored statistical outlier detection for anomaly-based network IDS, demonstrating that multivariate Gaussian models can effectively identify anomalous flows when the normal traffic distribution is sufficiently well-characterised. Khraisat et al.~\cite{khraisat2019survey} provided a comprehensive survey of anomaly detection frameworks, evaluating clustering, statistical, and neural network-based approaches against multiple criteria including detection rate, false-positive rate, computational efficiency, and generalisability.

\subsection{Isolation Forest}

The Isolation Forest algorithm~\cite{khraisat2019survey} has gained prominence as a practical unsupervised anomaly detector for high-dimensional tabular data. Unlike distance or density-based methods, Isolation Forest identifies anomalies by their ease of isolation: anomalous observations, being statistically unusual, are isolated in fewer random binary splits than typical normal observations. The anomaly score is inversely proportional to the average path length across an ensemble of random isolation trees. This mechanism makes Isolation Forest computationally efficient (linear time complexity), parameter-light, and naturally suited to high-dimensional feature spaces without requiring density estimation.

\section{Deep Learning in Cybersecurity}

\subsection{Deep Neural Networks for IDS}

The capacity of deep neural networks to learn hierarchical, abstract representations from raw or minimally preprocessed data has made them increasingly attractive for network intrusion detection tasks where manually engineered features may fail to capture complex attack signatures.

Javaid et al.~\cite{javaid2016deep} were among the first to systematically apply deep learning to network IDS, demonstrating that a five-layer deep autoencoder pre-trained in an unsupervised fashion and subsequently fine-tuned with supervised labels achieved significantly better detection accuracy than traditional ML algorithms on the NSL-KDD dataset. The key insight was that unsupervised pre-training enables the network to learn a compact, discriminative representation of network traffic features before labelled examples are introduced, reducing overfitting in the subsequent supervised training phase.

\subsection{Autoencoders for Anomaly Detection}

Shone et al.~\cite{shone2018deep} proposed a non-symmetric deep autoencoder (NDAE) combined with a random forest classifier for network intrusion detection. The NDAE learns a compressed latent representation of network flows in an unsupervised manner; flows that cannot be accurately reconstructed from their latent representation are flagged as anomalous. By combining this unsupervised representation learning with a supervised classifier operating on the latent features, the system achieves high detection rates for both known attack types and novel variants that differ substantially from the training distribution.

\subsection{Recurrent Networks and Temporal Modelling}

Yin et al.~\cite{yin2017rnn} demonstrated the application of recurrent neural networks, specifically Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units, to network intrusion detection. The key motivation is that network attacks often manifest as temporal patterns---sequences of related flows that individually appear benign but collectively reveal malicious intent. DDoS attacks, for example, involve sustained high-volume packet sequences; port scanning generates rapid sequential probe flows; and command-and-control botnet communication follows distinctive timing patterns. LSTM networks can learn these sequential dependencies through their gating mechanisms, which selectively retain relevant information across time steps. The study demonstrated superior performance on sequence-dependent attack categories compared to non-temporal classifiers, at the cost of substantially longer training times and greater sensitivity to hyperparameter choices.

Mohammadpour et al.~\cite{mohammadpour2022cnn} surveyed CNN-based approaches, showing that one-dimensional convolutional networks applied to feature vectors can capture local feature correlations with significantly fewer parameters than fully connected architectures, making them an efficient intermediate option between MLPs and LSTMs.

\subsection{Limitations of Deep Learning in IDS}

Despite their performance advantages, deep learning approaches in IDS suffer from several persistent limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data requirements:} Deep networks require large training sets to avoid overfitting; many real-world IDS datasets, particularly for rare attack types, contain insufficient labelled examples.
    \item \textbf{Computational cost:} Training and inference costs for large deep networks may be prohibitive for real-time deployment on commodity hardware.
    \item \textbf{Interpretability:} The black-box nature of deep networks makes it difficult to understand why a particular flow was classified as malicious, impeding analyst investigation and regulatory compliance.
    \item \textbf{Adversarial fragility:} Deep networks are known to be vulnerable to adversarial examples---carefully crafted perturbations of input features designed to cause misclassification~\cite{tafreshian2025adv}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hybrid and Ensemble Approaches}

\subsection{Motivation for Hybrid Architectures}

The complementary strengths and weaknesses of supervised and unsupervised methods motivate the development of hybrid architectures that combine multiple detection paradigms. Supervised classifiers achieve high accuracy on known attack types but cannot detect zero-day threats. Unsupervised anomaly detectors can flag novel patterns but generate excessive false positives. A well-designed hybrid system can exploit the precision of supervised classification for known threats while using unsupervised components to maintain sensitivity to novel variants.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods in IDS}

Khan et al.~\cite{khan2021invehicle} introduced a multi-tier ensemble integrating SVMs, decision trees, and logistic regression through a stacking meta-learner. The stacking architecture trains a second-level classifier to learn optimal weights for combining the predictions of the base classifiers, exploiting their complementary error patterns to reduce overall misclassification rates. Ring et al.~\cite{ring2019survey} explored dynamic ensemble selection, in which the most appropriate classifier from a pre-trained pool is selected at inference time based on the contextual properties of the incoming traffic sample. This adaptive mechanism demonstrated superior robustness to changing traffic distributions compared to fixed ensemble weights.

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness}

Tafreshian and Zhang~\cite{tafreshian2025adv} addressed the adversarial robustness of ML-based IDS systems, a concern that will become increasingly important as adversaries become aware of the detection methodologies used against them. Their defensive framework employs adversarial training---augmenting the training set with adversarially perturbed examples---combined with certified defence mechanisms that provide provable guarantees on the model's behaviour under bounded perturbations. Sewak et al.~\cite{sewak2022drl} reviewed the application of deep reinforcement learning to cybersecurity, demonstrating its potential for online, adaptive detection in dynamic threat environments where attack strategies evolve continuously in response to defensive actions.

\section{Research Gaps and Positioning}

Based on the literature reviewed, the following research gaps are identified:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Integrated preprocessing.} Few studies address class imbalance, feature redundancy, and data quality jointly within a single coherent pipeline. Most works treat these as separate concerns addressed in isolation.
    \item \textbf{Systematic multi-algorithm comparison.} Published comparative studies often evaluate a limited set of algorithms, use inconsistent experimental protocols, or report only a subset of relevant metrics (e.g., accuracy without F1-score or per-class breakdowns).
    \item \textbf{Operational interpretability.} While SHAP has been applied to cybersecurity tasks, its combined use with LIME for both global and local explanations in an IDS context, with explicit connection to analyst workflows, has not been comprehensively documented.
    \item \textbf{Deployment architecture.} The gap between benchmark performance and operational deployment remains largely unaddressed; few studies propose concrete SIEM-compatible architectures.
\end{enumerate}

The present research directly addresses all four gaps through its integrated methodology.

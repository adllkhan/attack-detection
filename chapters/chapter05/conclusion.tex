\section{Summary of Research}

This thesis described the end-to-end design and experimental validation of a comprehensive machine-learning framework for the analysis of network attack data that returns effective and comprehensible defence strategies for use against eithermanaged or emerging attacks. The inadequacy of classical signature-based intrusion detection methods has been acknowledged due to the serendipitous emergence of AI-powered self-learning attack methods, requiring additional investigation in the academic literature to provide preprocessing, systematic multi-algorithm comparisons, operational interpretability, and deployable system architectures.

The research involved five phases. The first phase screened through literature to identify candidate benchmark datasets, machine learning approaches, anomaly detection regimes, deep learning architectures, and hybrid ensemble systems for network intrusion detection. It was confirmed that gradient-boosting ensemble methods give the best performance on high-dimensionality tabular network traffic data, and the following four specific research gaps motivate the present work.

In phase two, the CICIDS2017 dataset was chosen as the primary experimental data source, being based on realistic generation of traffic, broad coverage of attack types, rich extraction of attack and normal features, and compelling previous work based on CICIDS2017 that allows explicit evaluations to antecedent works. The dataset was acquired, combined from multiple CSV sources, and passed through a comprehensive quality assessment that identified 23{,}415 records occurring more than once, 8{,}941 records with missing or infinite values, and labels with internal inconsistencies—all of which were remediated.

Experimental results support five key findings:

Firstly, the described preprocessing pipeline effectively removed noise and enabled subsequent machine learning models to identify meaningful patterns from the raw traffic data.
Secondly, the proposed ensemble method provides a significant improvement over the individual performance of each base learner.
Thirdly, the use of SMOTE oversampling and random undersampling has greatly improved the ability of the models to correctly classify instances of different types of attacks.
Fourthly, a number of the machine learning methods tested have achieved very high accuracy rates on the test set.
Lastly, although there have been some successes, there remains a need for further development in order to be able to detect certain forms of malicious activity.

A number of potential avenues exist for future work. These include developing more robust and flexible architectures for integrating various machine learning algorithms; improving the design and implementation of the initial preprocessing stage of the overall architecture; exploring alternative methods to tackle the problem of imbalanced class distributions; and employing additional methods for enhancing the interpretability of the predictions made by machine learning classifiers.

Further experimentation may help provide insight regarding whether these methods can be applied successfully in other contexts in which network intrusion detection is used.

These would likely involve extending experiments involving either synthetic or real-world datasets representing new threat vectors; testing new machine learning architectures; and experimenting with alternative architectures that utilize machine learning algorithms in combination with traditional signature-based anomaly detection systems.

As discussed previously, there are several difficulties linked with detecting complex threats in large-scale networks. Developing solutions that can deal with many of these issues will require continued research in areas such as machine learning, natural language processing, and computer vision.

Additionally, one of the primary reasons why machine learning based intrusion detection has seen increased interest is due to its potential to perform well when dealing with unknown threats. This is because they do not rely on pre-existing knowledge about known threats; instead, they learn from experience.

However, there are some important challenges to overcome before this potential becomes reality. For example, it is necessary to develop architectures capable of accurately identifying attacks without requiring large amounts of labeled training data.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{LightGBM is able to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the CICIDS2017 benchmark: } LightGBM’s accuracy was approximately ~0.98, F1 score was approximately ~0.97 and its Receiver Operating Characteristic Area Under Curve (ROC AUC) was approximately ~0.99. Each of these were found to be superior to those of five other competing algorithms that were also tested. In addition, LightGBM was by far the quickest algorithm to fit the entire gradient-boosting model (approximately 89 seconds), as compared to the XGBoost version of this model which took approximately 317 seconds. This therefore serves as additional proof of the real benefits that are provided by leaf-wise tree-up growth and GOSS sampling in conjunction with EFB feature bundling for the network traffic classification problem.
\item \textbf{Preprocessing makes a difference.} Qualitative improvement in minority class recall occurred when moving from the raw, imbalanced dataset to the preprocessed training distribution, for all models. Using SMOTE and undersampling improved the balance of both individually and through the "stacked" reductions, resulted in an average decrease of 23% in false positives for the Botnet, Brute Force, Web Attack categories, etc., relative to models trained using the unbalanced original data. \begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Feature selection makes for better generalisability.} By not playing on the worst models trained on all the features, we know that more noise than signal was added for the omitted features. The gain observed for the feature-less versions of the model was greatest for the KNN classifiers (F1 gain of 0.04) and Logistic Regression (F1 gain of 0.03), as would be expected of models more sensitive to irrelevant features.
\item \textbf{Interpretability builds on domain-consistent feature importance returned.} Flow Duration, InPktRate, OutByteRate, Bwd Packet Length Max and Source Port turning out to be the five most discriminative features were known to signify malicious behaviour in the network and by a known Cyber Security grounding means interpreted this way, it imbued it the prescience it bases at activating on this domain on, i.e. it will thus behave acceptably when it is live activated.
\item \textbf{Hybrid architectures broaden zero day detection ability.} The unsupervised mode of the Isolation Forest model acheived meaningful detection on 73% of the attack instances, demonstrating a zero-day discrimination ability that is worth feeding into our supervised classifier. The temporal LSTM model benefitted from grouping attacks (like DDoS, PortScan, etc.) 2 percentage points in being able to detect them more contributionally than per-flow attacked models, showing how important the temporal context of attacks is for multi-step attack detection.
\item \textbf{The hypothesis at the core of this research is characterised correct, it being borne out in light of this contribution’s core logic.} That ML methods \& data science principles plied methodically to classifying network traffic produces a system that exceeds the thresholds that were very much anticipated for the measures ($F_1 > 0.95$, $\mathrm{AUC} > 0.98$) and uniquely transparent to an analyst in operations for the purpose of explainable decision making.
\end{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Concrete SIEM-compatible deployment architecture.} We underpin our recommendations for how a future IDS might look by tackling the gap between developing our model and deploying with industry tools, dedicating our discussion to data ingestion, classification engine, interpretability module, and platform integration.
